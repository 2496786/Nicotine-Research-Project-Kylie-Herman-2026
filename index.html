About the Drug

What is nicotine, what do people call it, and is it legal?

What is nicotine?

Nicotine is a drug found naturally in the tobacco plant and in small amounts in some other nightshades like tamatoes and eggplants. In the amounts found in tobacco and vape products, nicotine is a stimulant drug — it speeds up signals between the brain and body. Pure nicotine is a colorless oily liquid that is toxic when concentrated.

Nic (as in "a nic buzz")

Legal or illegal?

Legal — with rules

In Canada, nicotine products (cigarettes, cigars, vapes, smokeless tobacco) are legal to buy and use if you are 19 or older. Most other countries also allow nicotine to be sold but set a minimum age of 18-21 with heavy tax.

Restricted or banned

Some countries (like India, Thailand, Brazil, and Australia for non prescription versions) ban or heavily restrict the sale of e-cigarettes. Selling nicotine to minors is illegal everywhere in Canada.

Addiction

2 out of 3 people who try smoking once become daily smokers, and most adult smokers say they want to quit but can't.

  1. Reason 1

    Reaches the brain fast

    From one hit, nicotine reaches the brain in ~10–20 seconds. Fast acting drugs are more addictive.

  2. Reason 2

    it hijacks dopamine

    Nicotine forces dopamine release in the reward pathway. Your brain learns to repeat actions that feel good

  3. Reason 3

    Tolerance builds fast

    The brain grows more nicotine receptors to . You end up needing more nicotine to feel the same effects, So you use more.

  4. Reason 4

    Withdrawal feels bad

    When the nicotine wears off, your extra receptors turn lonely. People may feel irritable, anxious, distracted, and have cravings.

  5. Reason 5

    daily routines

    Smoking after meals, vaping on your breaks, dipping while driving, all these habits build routines that trigger cravings even without thinking.

symptoms:

Cravings
Irritability
Anxiety
Trouble concentrating
Headaches
Restlessness
Increased appetite
Trouble sleeping
Depressed mood

(Most symptoms peak in the first week and fade over 2–4 weeks.)

In the Brain

What happens to neurotransmitters when nicotine is introduced.

Neurotransmitters effected:

Dopamine
Reward, motivation, pleasure
Acetylcholine
Attention, memory, muscle signaling
Adrenaline
Heart rate, energy, fight or flight
  1. 1

    Nicotine reaches the brain

    Within ~10–20 seconds after inhalation, nicotine penetrates the blood to brain barrier.

  2. 2

    It binds to nAChRs

    Nicotine sticks to receptors called "nicotinic acetylcholine receptors" (nAChRs). These are usually meant respond to a natural chemical called acetylcholine.

  3. 3

    Dopamine is released

    Activating those receptors (nAChRs) triggers neurons in the brain's reward pathway to release dopamine, the "feel good" neurotransmitter/hormone.

  4. 4

    Adrenaline joins the party

    Nicotine also signals the adrenal glands to release adrenaline (aka epinephrine), which raises heart rate, bp, and breathing rate. That's the 'head rush', 'headie' or 'buzz' feeling.

  5. 5

    The brain remembers

    Your brain links the dopamine reward to whatever you were doing (smoking, vaping).

Why it snowballs worse over time

With repetative use of nicotine, the brain grows more nAChRs to handle the flooding of nicotine — this is called upregulation. When nicotine wears off, all the extra receptors become empty, and the brain feels un-balanced. That's called withdrawal, and it makes people want to use again.

Effects on the Body

Short-term hits in minutes. Long-term damage builds over time.

Short term (minutes to hours)

Physical

  • •Faster heart rate
  • •Higher blood pressure
  • •Head rush
  • •Decreased appetite
  • •Cold hands and feet

Mental

  • •Feeling alert / awake
  • •Mild euphoria / 'buzz'
  • •Better focus
  • •Reduced stress
Long term (months to years)

Physical

  • •Lung disease
  • •Higher risk of heart attack and stroke
  • •cancer
  • •Gum disease
  • •Damage to blood vessels
  • •Bad pregnancy

Mental

  • •addiction & cravings
  • •anxiety and depression
  • •Trouble focusing without nicotine
  • •In teens: lasting damage to attention, memory, and learning (the brain is still developing)
Note: All of the bad long term physical side-effects (cancer, lung disease) come from the other chemicals in tobacco and vape smoke — tar, c0(carbon monoxide), metals, and formaldehyde — not from nicotine.

Medicinal Uses

Nicotine has real medical uses.

Nicotine Replacement Therapy (NRT)

NRT gives the body a light, constant dose of nicotine without the harm of smoke or vapes. The aim is to remove withdrawal and cravings while a person quits tobacco. "NRT roughly doubles a person's chances of quitting successfully."

  • Patches
  • Gum
  • Lozenges
  • Nasal sprays
  • Inhalers
Active research areas

Scientists have found nicotine (or simular drugs) to help for several conditions:

  • • Parkinson's disease — smokers have reduced rates of Parkinson's, so researchers are testing whether nicotine helps protect dopamine neurons.
  • • Alzheimer's disease — studies suggest nicotine patches help with attention and memory.
  • • Ulcerative colitis — nicotine helps reduce inflammation in the colon.
  • • ADHD & Tourette syndrome — studies show nicotine's possitive effects on attention and tics.

How It Enters the Body

Inhaled, absorbed, or ingested?

Inhaled

Smoking cigarettes, cigars, hookah or vapes. Nicotine goes from from the lungs into the bloodstream and reaches the brain in ~10–20 seconds, faster than an IV.

  • Cigarettes
  • Cigars
  • Vapes / "e-cigarettes'
  • Hookah

Absorbed (mouth/skin)

Smokeless tobacco like chew, dip, or snus sits between the gum and cheek. nicotine is absorbed through the veins of the mouth. Patches absorb nicotine through the skin slowly over a longer time.

  • Chewing tobacco
  • Dip / snus
  • Nicotine pouches (Zyn)
  • Skin patches
  • Gum & lozenges

Ingestion (rare)

Nicotine is rarely swallowed on purpose because the stomach destroys most of it. Accidentaly swallowing vape juice by children is a poisoning risk and can be posably deadly.

  • Accidental swallowing of vape juice
  • Swallowed chewing tobacco juice

Speed matters for addiction. The faster a drug reaches the brain, the more addictive it is. That's why vapes and cigarettes are so addictive
